// Real-World Tariff Examples — Why Some Trade Rules Are Ridiculous
US Chicken Tax on Light Trucks
25%
United States · Since 1963
Originally a retaliation against European restrictions on US chicken imports, this tariff has persisted for 60+ years and now mainly protects American truck manufacturers. Foreign automakers route around it by importing trucks as cargo vehicles, then converting them domestically.
⚠ Outlived its purpose by 6 decades and counting.
EU Banana Import Tariff
€114/t
European Union · Ongoing
The EU imposes a fixed tariff on banana imports from non-ACP countries (Latin America) to protect preferential trade agreements with former colonies. This led to a 20-year "Banana War" dispute at the WTO, raising prices for European consumers while distorting global trade flows.
⚠ A 20-year trade war over fruit. Seriously.
Japan's Rice Tariff
778%
Japan · Ongoing
Japan applies an almost prohibitive tariff on imported rice to protect domestic farmers. At 778%, imported rice becomes economically impossible to sell competitively, effectively blocking the market entirely while Japanese consumers pay some of the world's highest rice prices.
⚠ 778% is not a tariff. It's a wall.
US Import Tariff on Peanut Butter
132%
United States · Ongoing
The US imposes a 132% tariff on peanut butter imports beyond a small quota, effectively shutting out foreign competition. Despite this, major peanut butter producers like Canada continue to export via quota allocations, while US consumers pay inflated prices for a sandwich staple.
⚠ A 132% markup on sandwiches since forever.
// Can a True Autarky Exist?

Autarky is the concept of a fully self-sufficient nation — one that neither imports nor exports, producing everything it needs domestically. In theory, this would mean zero trade. The gravity model would output 0 for any pair involving an autarky.

Currently, no true autarky exists. North Korea comes closest — heavily sanctioned, with minimal formal trade — yet still engages in informal cross-border trade with China and Russia. Even the most isolated economies need something from the outside world, whether fuel, medicine, or food.

The closest historical example of a near-autarky succeeding short-term was Albania under Enver Hoxha (1976–1991), which constitutionally banned all foreign loans, investment, and aid. It collapsed economically shortly after the policy ended. A post-scarcity world — like Wakanda — where technology eliminates all resource constraints is the only realistic scenario where true autarky could function sustainably.
