International Trade

Gravity Model of Trade

The gravity model predicts bilateral trade flows between two countries. Based on Newton's gravitational law, it states that trade volume is proportional to the product of two economies' sizes and inversely proportional to the distance between them. The formula: T = A × (GDP_A × GDP_B) / D.

International Trade

Tariffs & Trade Policy

A tariff is a tax on imported goods. Countries use tariffs to protect domestic industries, generate revenue, or retaliate against trade partners. TradeFlow covers four real-world tariff examples — including a 778% rice tariff and a 25% truck tariff that outlived its purpose by 60 years.

  • What makes a tariff "ridiculous"
  • How tariffs distort trade flows
  • Historical context for real tariffs
International Trade

Autarky — Can It Exist?

Autarky is full national self-sufficiency — no imports, no exports. In theory, the gravity model outputs 0 trade for an autarkic nation. In practice, no true autarky has ever been sustained. TradeFlow covers the historical attempts and why they failed.

  • What autarky means in trade theory
  • Albania's near-autarky experiment
  • Post-scarcity as the only viable scenario
Management Science

Linear Programming

LP is a mathematical method for finding the best outcome — maximum profit or minimum cost — subject to a set of constraints. It is foundational to operations research and management science. TradeFlow lets you build and solve LP problems visually.

  • Standard LP notation (Sets, Parameters, Variables)
  • Writing objective functions and constraints
  • Graphical solution and feasible region
  • Corner point theorem — why the optimal is always at a corner

Gamification in Education

Gamification applies game mechanics — points, levels, narrative, challenge — to non-game contexts to increase engagement and motivation. In the classroom, it reframes learning as a problem to overcome rather than content to memorize.

Logistics

TLC vs SCM

The Transportation-Logistics Chain (TLC) covers the internal movement of goods: Transport → Storage → Inventory → Distribution. Supply Chain Management (SCM) is broader — it wraps around the TLC and includes suppliers and customers at each end. A common exam mistake is placing SCM inside the TLC.

// FAQ

Common questions.

Everything you need to know before diving in.

Completely free, no sign-up needed. TradeFlow runs entirely in your browser — there's no server, no database, no account. Open a page and start using it. It will always be free and open source.
The calculator implements the basic gravity model formula: T = A × (GDP_A × GDP_B) / D. This is the standard textbook version used in introductory trade theory courses. Real-world gravity models are more complex — they account for trade agreements, cultural proximity, currency, colonial history, and more. The output is a trade potential index, not an actual dollar value of trade. Use it to understand the concept and compare relative trade relationships.
The optimizer uses the corner point theorem — a fundamental result in linear programming that states the optimal solution always occurs at a corner (vertex) of the feasible region. The tool enumerates all corner points of the feasible region formed by your constraints, evaluates the objective function at each, and returns the one with the highest profit. This is equivalent to the graphical method taught in introductory MS courses.
The TLC (Transportation-Logistics Chain) describes the internal logistics flow of goods: from the moment they leave a production point to the moment they reach a distribution end point. SCM (Supply Chain Management) is a broader management framework that also includes the supplier at the start and the customer at the end — both of which are external to the logistics operation itself. SCM coordinates across the entire value chain; the TLC is one part of that chain.
Bananas are actually a perfect product for studying trade and logistics. They are one of the most internationally traded agricultural commodities, they require specific cold-chain logistics (temperature-controlled transport and storage), they are subject to real trade disputes (the EU Banana War lasted 20 years at the WTO), and they have a clear, simple supply chain that maps neatly onto the TLC. They also happen to have been used as the example product in the course that inspired this tool.

Try changing the inputs, see what happens, and make sure you understand why the results change. The tools are most useful when you use them to ask "what if" questions.
